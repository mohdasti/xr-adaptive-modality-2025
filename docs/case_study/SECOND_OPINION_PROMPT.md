## The Prompt (Copy Everything Below the Line)

---

You are an expert UX Research portfolio reviewer and hiring manager. I'm writing a UX-Research focused case study for my portfolio, and I need a **second opinion** on my detailed outline before I start drafting.

**Context:**
- I conducted a research project on **adaptive input modality systems in Extended Reality (XR)** — specifically, can context-aware UI adaptations reduce gaze failure modes (e.g., Midas Touch slips) and workload under time pressure?
- I built a remote web-based research platform, collected data from ~80 participants, and analyzed performance, workload (NASA-TLX), and qualitative debrief data.
- I already have a **technical** case study published (methods-heavy, with statistical models, LBA analysis, etc.). This **new** version should be **user-research centric** — less technical, more emphasis on user pain points, insights, and design recommendations.
- **Target audience:** UX Research recruiters, AR/XR product teams, design leadership. They should understand *how I think* and *what I learned about users*.

**What I'm asking you to do:**
Please review the attached outline (`UX_CASE_STUDY_OUTLINE.md`) and provide a structured second opinion. Structure your response as follows:

---

### Part 1: Overall Assessment (200–300 words)

- Is the proposed section order (Problem → Solution → Context → Key Learnings → Future Directions → Reflections) optimal for a UX Research case study? Should anything be reordered, merged, or split?
- Does the outline strike the right balance between rigor and accessibility for a non-technical UX audience?
- What is the single strongest aspect of the outline? What is the single weakest or most risky aspect?

---

### Part 2: Section-by-Section Critique

For each of the six main sections, provide:

1. **Strengths** — What works well? What will resonate with recruiters?
2. **Gaps or concerns** — What's missing? What could backfire or confuse readers?
3. **Suggestions** — One or two concrete improvements (e.g., "Add a subsection on participant recruitment challenges" or "Consider moving X to Y section").

Sections to review:
- Section 1: The Problem  
- Section 2: The Solution  
- Section 3: Context & Background  
- Section 4: Key Learnings (Insights & Recommendations)  
- Section 5: Future Directions & Industry Applications  
- Section 6: Reflections  

---

### Part 3: Alignment with UX Case Study Best Practices

Based on your knowledge of successful UX Research case studies and portfolio best practices:

- Does the outline follow a **storytelling arc** (e.g., Problem → Journey → Insight → Impact) rather than a dry report format?
- Is the **"So what?"** for each section clear? Would a recruiter skim and understand the value?
- Are **limitations and failures** (e.g., width inflation not activating, modest declutter effect) handled in a way that builds trust rather than undermines credibility?
- Is the **Reflections** section sufficiently deep to demonstrate critical thinking and professional growth?

---

### Part 4: Specific Questions

Please answer these explicitly:

1. **Problem section:** Should the opening hook be scenario-based (Option A) or contrast-based (Option B)? Or do you recommend a different approach?
2. **Solution vs. Context:** The outline puts Solution before Context. Some case studies put "What I Built" / Methods first. Which order do you recommend for a UX Research audience, and why?
3. **Key Learnings:** The outline has five insights. Are they ordered correctly by impact? Should any be combined, dropped, or elevated?
4. **Reflections:** Is "What I Would Do Differently" the right tone? Some portfolios avoid it for fear of sounding negative. Your take?
5. **Length:** The outline is detailed. When written out, should this be a ~1,500-word "portfolio highlight" or a ~3,000+ word "deep dive"? What's the sweet spot for UX Research case studies in 2025?

---

### Part 5: Optional Additions

Based on the outline, are there any **sections, subsections, or elements** you would add that are currently missing? Examples: participant quotes, before/after comparison, competitive landscape, stakeholder feedback, iteration story, etc.

---

**Please be direct and constructive.** I want actionable feedback, not generic praise. If something doesn't work, say so and suggest an alternative.
